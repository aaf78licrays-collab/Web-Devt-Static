Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about wildlife conservation, ethical wildlife viewing, and how you can make a difference in protecting our planet's precious species.

Responsible wildlife viewing is crucial for animal welfare and conservation. Always maintain a safe distance from animals - use binoculars or telephoto lenses for closer views. Never feed wildlife as this disrupts their natural behavior and diet, and can make them dependent on humans. Minimize noise and sudden movements to avoid stressing animals. Always stay on marked paths and trails to protect sensitive habitats and avoid disturbing nesting or breeding areas. Follow all park regulations and guidelines, and consider hiring local, certified guides who understand animal behavior and conservation principles. Remember, observing wildlife should never come at the expense of their wellbeing.
Habitat loss is the single biggest threat to global wildlife populations. As human populations expand, forests are cleared for agriculture, urban development, and infrastructure, leaving animals with fragmented and diminished living spaces. Climate change compounds this issue by altering ecosystems faster than many species can adapt, changing migration patterns, food availability, and breeding cycles. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and extreme weather events are forcing species to relocate or face extinction. Additionally, deforestation, pollution, and ocean acidification are destroying critical habitats worldwide. These interconnected threats require urgent global action to prevent mass extinctions.
We offer multiple ways for you to support wildlife conservation. For donations, visit our Donation Page where you can make one-time or recurring contributions. Every donation directly funds anti-poaching patrols, habitat restoration, veterinary care, and community education programs. Several species face critical endangerment. African Rhinos, particularly the Black and Northern White Rhino, are severely threatened by poaching for their horns, which are illegally sold on black markets. Despite intensive protection efforts, rhino populations remain dangerously low. Orangutans in Southeast Asia face habitat destruction from palm oil plantations and logging. These intelligent great apes have lost over 80% of their natural habitat in the past 20 years. Other critically endangered species include the Amur Leopard, Sumatran Elephant, Hawksbill Turtle, and Mountain Gorilla. Each species faces unique challenges including poaching, habitat loss, human-wildlife conflict, and climate change. Conservation efforts focus on habitat protection, anti-poaching measures, breeding programs, and community engagement.
Ethical wildlife photography prioritizes animal welfare over getting the perfect shot. Use telephoto lenses (300mm or longer) to photograph animals from a safe distance without disturbing them or encroaching on their space. Never use bait, calls, or other methods to lure animals for photos, as this disrupts natural behavior and can put them at risk. Avoid flash photography, which can startle animals, damage their night vision, or reveal their location to predators. Never alter the environment by moving vegetation, nests, or other habitat features. Respect breeding and nesting seasons by keeping extra distance. Be patient and allow animals to behave naturally - forced interactions create stress. Share your photos responsibly, avoiding location details that could lead to harassment of animals or sensitive sites. Remember, the wellbeing of the animal always comes before the photograph.
